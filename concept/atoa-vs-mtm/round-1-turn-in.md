**Synthesizer:** Agent 12 (Wave 3) | **Date:** 2026-04-27 | **Inputs:** Wave 1 (10 production agents) + Wave 2 (Devil's Advocate). Treat this as the load-bearing R1 artifact for downstream rounds.

## Executive read

The swarm converges on a coherent product-shape question that survives adversarial pressure but only barely: the SC1/WC3 lobby gap is real and creator-mourned, the seven-mode-types-at-launch posture is the genre's most direct anti-pattern response to the documented kill mechanisms (mid-dev pivot, underbaked launch, thin content), and the Battle Pass / Ranked surface is simultaneously a known revenue lane and the most-detected community-trust landmine in the corpus. The Devil's Advocate pass exposes that the convergence on "lobby gap" rests on a smaller source substrate than its five-agent agreement implies; that the swarm sampled disproportionately on the dependent variable for failure-pattern claims; that AtoA's own current pivot is structurally adjacent to the kill mechanism Agent 6 surfaced; and that the Stormgate-AtoA resource asymmetry ($36M vs solo) is a quality-bar question the swarm under-examined. The reconciled HTML-prototype number is materially below the headline 65%. The strongest unresolved tension is whether seven-mode breadth is a differentiator (Agent 4) or a multiplied execution risk (Agents 6, 8, DA Section B-3).

**Load-bearing observations:**

- The lobby gap is unfilled because the *substrate* (host cost, creator labor scaffold, IP-friendly EULA, replay/observer/snapshot tooling) was never replicated standalone — not because the *gameplay* was hard.
- Battle Pass anger is most reliably triggered by *change* relative to a free baseline (Agent 8) — but Agent 6 attacks the model itself when grafted onto co-op/PvE; these disagree and the synthesis cannot bury the disagreement.
- "Polish-complete at launch" is the immune response to Stormgate / Defenders-of-Ardania / Tower-Wars-2012 — but the resource gap to clear that bar with seven mode-types at solo scope is the largest unexamined assumption.
- Creator coverage is structurally suppressed for TW (Agent 9) yet awaits novelty (Agent 4); the contradiction means creator-tooling investment is a *bet*, not a known-good lever.
- HTML prototype: ~65% pure-logic preserved is the optimist read; Agent 7's stub catalogue (fusion execution, ability targeting, cosmetics, lineage drift) lands the honest reconciled figure closer to 50–55% *deliverable*, with the remainder being either rebuild or spec-not-yet-written.
- The current pivot (free HTML → premium engine successor) is itself a mid-dev pivot per Agent 6's own taxonomy — the synthesis must reckon explicitly, not silently.
- Promise 2's "lobby substrate cost" is the expensive piece that historically only Blizzard-subsidized games carried; the funding mechanism for a standalone solo title is unsolved.

## Reconciled HTML-audit number

Agent 1 reports ~65% of demonstrable progress preserved on engine port, with a clean optimism-leaning split (65% pure-logic / 35% rebuild). Agent 7 surfaces five concrete delivery gaps: Fusion execution = stub toast (no God spawn), commander signature ability = button with "targeting TBD" and no spec, cosmetic unlocks = dead-end clicks, five lineages obsoleted to three civs without prototype reflecting that rev, and stale "11-age" copy in mode-select. These are not rendering or transport-layer items — they are *concept-fidelity* and *spec-completeness* gaps that Agent 1's pure-logic / rendering taxonomy does not surface.

**Reconciled honest figure: ~50–55% of demonstrable progress preserved as port-ready logic, with a further ~10–15% present-but-spec-blocked (cannot port until design specifies what the button does), and the remaining ~30–35% as rendering/transport rebuild.** The optimism in 65% comes from counting stubbed-but-structurally-present features (fusion menu, ability button, cosmetic chips) as preserved logic; once Agent 7's stub catalogue is netted out, the deliverable-logic floor drops by roughly a third of the headline preserved share. This is not a port-cost catastrophe — Agent 1's identification of the balance/derivation/data layer as fully portable is independently true — but the headline 65% should not stand uncalibrated as "demonstrable progress."

## Promise-1 findings — coverage of all TW mode-types at launch

### Validations

BTD6's sustained 97%-tier sentiment is repeatedly attributed to *content-surface breadth* (modes, races, bosses, co-op, odysseys) rather than mechanical reinvention (Findings 1-02-1, 1-04-4) — direct external evidence that breadth-as-strategy can win. Creator-side framing identifies the multi-mode coverage thesis as *uncontested* in the standalone TW market: every recent hybrid grafts only one axis (RTS, factory, roguelite), leaving the seven-mode posture genuinely vacant (Finding 1-04-5, 1-04-6). The Three Moves Ahead "design-locked TD genre" framing identifies that whoever ships a structurally novel TW receives a free narrative tailwind (Finding 1-09-10). Stormgate's launch-incomplete failure is the strongest cautionary precedent for the alternative — markets actively punish thin-launch postures, validating the "polish-complete at launch" framing Promise 1 stakes (Finding 1-08-3).

### Warnings

Agent 6's morgue surfaces that *under-baked launches in this genre have no recovery path* (Finding 1-06-9) — and seven mode-types multiplies the surface where under-baked is detectable. Defenders of Ardania (Finding 1-05-08, 1-06-2) shipped the WC3-PvP-TW shape without lobby mutators or content depth and is the named cautionary precedent. Sanctum 2's explicit dev lesson is to *cut adjacent systems, not stack them* (Finding 1-03-1) — Promise 1 stacks adjacent mode-types by design. Iron Marines' "three tower types isn't a lot of choice" reception (Finding 1-02-11) demonstrates that hybrid TD/RTS games with limited vocabulary lose replay sentiment fast even at high production values. **DA reckoning required:** Agent 6 says mid-dev pivots are top-3 killer; AtoA's current pivot *is* a mid-dev pivot (Section D-2 of DA pass). And the Stormgate $36M-vs-solo-dev asymmetry (DA C-1 of required reckoning) means the resource posture to clear "polish-complete at all seven mode-types" is the single most unexamined assumption in the swarm.

## Promise-2 findings — SC1/WC3 lobby recognizability + customizability + novelty

### Validations

This is the densest convergence in the swarm. Reddit/Steam community sentiment names the WC3 custom-lobby experience as the genre's most-cited unmet wish (Finding 1-02-2, 1-02-12). Legion TD 2's creators explicitly credit the WC3 World Editor's *accessibility* — not power — as the durable retention engine (Finding 1-03-5). Creator longform converges on WC3/SC1 customs as a uniquely unreplicated phenomenon (Findings 1-04-2, 1-04-3, 1-04-6). Competitor teardown identifies host-configurable lobby mutators as the single most consistent mechanic SC1/WC3 customs had that no standalone reproduced except partially Legion TD 2 (Finding 1-05-01). Creator-side complaint atlas identifies the SC1/WC3 lobby pipeline as the single most-mourned missing feature (Finding 1-09-1) and Reforged's IP-grab EULA as the watershed creator betrayal (Finding 1-09-2). Stormgate's deferred map editor is the active anti-pattern (Finding 1-08-4).

### Warnings

Agent 6's tombstones identify lobby/community substrate cost as the *expensive* piece, historically only viable because Blizzard subsidized hosting (Finding 1-06-8). Tower Wars (Steam, 2012) is the precedent: 71% positive reviews, dead matchmaking pool, killed by absent host/browse substrate (Finding 1-06-4). SC2 Arcade's structural failure shows editor power without lobby/discovery UX rotted the audience (Finding 1-04-3). Creators identify the WC3-nostalgic cohort as 32-42-year-olds with 90 minutes weekly — the rhetorical mass may exceed the addressable audience (DA Section A-6, A-11). Account-deletion / IP / hostbot policy footguns trigger Steam-review nukes regardless of in-game design (Findings 1-08-20, 1-09-7).

### Convergence callout (5 agents)

**CONVERGENCE FINDING — "the SC1/WC3 lobby gap is the genre's most-cited unmet wish":** Agents 02, 03, 04, 05, 09 independently surface this. **DA counter (Section C-6):** the five agents cite a remarkably overlapping source substrate — Hive Workshop threads, the 2-3 PC Gamer / Massively-OP articles, the same Legion-TD-2 dev interviews, the canonical "Blizzard Killed Custom Maps" forum thread. Five agents reading three sources is source-amplification, not five-source triangulation. **Synthesizer's read:** the convergence is real but strength-downgraded from "five independent agents" to "five agents echoing a dominant 3-4-source discourse." The finding is still load-bearing — the lobby gap exists — but the *audience size* implied by the convergence is overstated, and R2 framing should test the gap-size question, not the gap-existence question.

## Promise-3 findings — optional Battle Pass + Ranked layered without compromising regular play

### Validations

Legion TD 2 sustains 87%+ positive sentiment with 800+ daily concurrent on a ranked-PvP-co-op TW with monthly esports — proving a ranked-progression niche exists in the exact mode-shape (Finding 1-02-3). BTD6 demonstrates that meta-progression layered on a frozen rule-set sustains long-tail engagement (Finding 1-05-09). Cosmetic-only monetization is consistently positively-received when explicitly called non-pay-to-win (Finding 1-02-4). FINDING-1-08-23 (cross-case across PvZ2, BTD6, Underlords) identifies that monetization-anger is keyed to *change* from a free baseline, not to *existence* — implying day-1-visible BP avoids the documented anger vector.

### Warnings

This is the densest *negative* convergence in the swarm. Sanctum 2's pre-committed Season Pass became a flexibility-killer (Finding 1-03-2). OMD!U + Dungeon Defenders 2 + Prime World Defenders all died on F2P/MTX models grafted onto co-op/PvE TW gameplay (Finding 1-06-1) — the pattern is near-perfect. Dungeon Defenders 2 reversed an explicit no-MTX promise and never recovered goodwill (Finding 1-06-7). Dota Underlords lost its core audience after Season 2 paywalled starting heroes (Finding 1-08-11). PvZ2 international's 95%+ negative-sentiment progression retrofit is the strongest "monetization grafted onto premium loop" cautionary tale (Finding 1-08-12). Battle Pass FOMO complaints are dominated by casual cohorts feeling time-budget squeeze (Finding 1-08-13) — solo-first AtoA implicitly targets exactly this demographic. Legion TD 2's smurf-paired-with-noob ranked pathology and 180-day-account-deletion policy are documented Promise-3 attack surfaces (Findings 1-08-7, 1-08-20). **Direct contradiction surfaced:** Finding 1-08-23 (anger keyed to change, fine if day-1) vs Finding 1-06-1 (model itself wrong for game shape regardless of timing). The synthesis cannot accept both unmodified.

## Player Complaint Atlas (preserved verbatim from Agent 8)

Agent 8's twelve frequency-ranked taxonomy buckets, preserved as the standalone artifact PM flagged as "gold." Each bucket is the player-voice grievance register, sampled from Steam negative-review aggregators, Steam Community threads, Hive Workshop, Blizzard official forums, ResetEra, EA forums, and gaming-press post-mortems.

**Rank 1 — Balance / power-creep / meta treadmill.** Universal across every TD; very-high frequency, high severity. Every patch cycle is a complaint cycle. Bloons TD 6 has publicly committed to "more targeted nerfs and fewer buff changes" in response (Finding 1-08-9). Combinatorial surface for AtoA: 9 Fusion Gods × 3 commanders × 3 civs is a wide attack surface for power-creep.

**Rank 2 — Monetization creep on previously-free content.** Very-high frequency, very-high severity. The unifying anger pattern is monetization *introduced after* a free / earned baseline existed (Finding 1-08-23). BTD6 retro-gating features ("Ninja Kiwi slowly becoming EA"), PvZ2 international, Underlords Season 2, Stormgate's $12.99 commander launch-day cash-shop hero are the canonical cases.

**Rank 3 — Matchmaking / smurfs / toxic lobbies.** High frequency, very-high severity in multiplayer-skewed TDs. Legion TD 2's most-upvoted negative reviews cluster on global-chat racism, smurf-driven matchmaking, and steep paywall-adjacent learning curve, with high-ranked players running 5+ smurf accounts to dodge MMR (Findings 1-08-1, 1-08-7).

**Rank 4 — Endgame / content cliff / repetition.** They Are Billions repeating-patterns-after-50% complaints; Element TD 2 wave-63-65 cheese-element cliff plus desync; Kingdom Rush 5 DLC reskinning and "started feeling incredibly similar after about three runs" (Findings 1-08-6, 1-08-10, 1-08-18).

**Rank 5 — Scope: launched feeling incomplete / half-baked modes.** Stormgate's self-confessed "we released far too early" is the strongest cautionary precedent for Promise 1 (Finding 1-08-3). Reforged's launch incompleteness is the long-tail tail-cousin.

**Rank 6 — Onboarding too steep / tutorial dumps everything.** RTS-wide; Element TD 2's "TOO COMPLEX" cohort; "modern games dump 40 mechanics on you, 15 hotkeys in the tutorial, new players close the game" (Findings 1-08-14, 1-08-19). Heritage-campaign-as-tutorial is the named missing element.

**Rank 7 — Customization deficit.** Heritage-cohort high-severity. WC3 Reforged copyright land-grab; Stormgate map editor delayed past launch (Findings 1-08-4, 1-08-5).

**Rank 8 — Bug rot / engine rot / abandoned support.** WC3 Reforged, SC2 arcade hacking ("infected" Direct Strike map versions wiping data — Finding 1-08-16), Underlords abandoned.

**Rank 9 — UX / UI / accessibility.** Stormgate combat audio "no weight," Simu Liu dialogue inaudible — polish-tier complaints with disproportionate review-score impact (Finding 1-08-22). Generic TD colorblind issues.

**Rank 10 — Battle-pass FOMO / treadmill obligation.** Casual-cohort high-severity. Time-budget squeeze (3 months → 8 weeks coupled with growing reward tiers) is the predatory vector, not the price (Finding 1-08-13). Underlords S2 acute case.

**Rank 11 — Pricing vs content delivered.** Stormgate $12.99 vs SC2 $6.25 commander; cash-shop hero on launch day did more reputational damage than gameplay shortfalls (Finding 1-08-15).

**Rank 12 — Other footguns: account deletion, IP land-grab, save-system absence.** Legion TD 2 180-day-deletion policy, Reforged EULA, Stormgate no-save campaign, Wintermaul "the only way to lose is to lag out," 12-unit selection limit preserved as identity-defining-or-not heritage debate (Findings 1-08-17, 1-08-20, 1-08-24). Triggered rarely but Steam-review-nuking when triggered.

**DA epistemic attack on this atlas (Section C-1):** Steam negative reviews sample the angry; forum threads sample the engaged-and-unhappy; the silent majority of TW players who installed, played 8 hours, and churned without comment are unsampled. The atlas is empirically a complaint distribution among complainants, not a risk distribution among players. **Synthesizer's read:** the atlas is correctly used as worst-case-flagging (warranted), not as risk-weighted design driver (unwarranted without silent-majority calibration). This downgrade applies to all Promise-3 inferences derived from it.

## Strongest Alternative Paths (R2 Arguer B ammunition)

### Alternative 1 — Paid Early Access, single product (Shape F)

**The strongest case against the current sequential free-then-premium pivot.** Paid EA outperformed free EA on every observable engagement metric in 2024 (38.9h median playtime vs 27.1h, 86% vs 81% review score, 23.67% of 2024 indie revenue) per Finding 1-10-1. The reference class — Hades, Hades 2, Vampire Survivors, Manor Lords, They Are Billions — clusters tightly around the unfinished-but-shippable strategy-game niche AtoA inhabits. EA explicitly accommodates incomplete mode coverage as a known-state, not a broken promise; converts wishlist intent to paid commitment immediately rather than deferring monetization to a successor product that may never ship; and attracts the modder/customizer demographic that maps to Promise 2's audience. Stormgate's failure case (Finding 1-10-2) is *free* EA at $36M+ funding peaking under 1k concurrent — direct counter-evidence that "free for discoverability" did not solve the problem free-launch is supposed to solve, while paid-EA conversion friction filters for the high-intent cohort that drives Steam's review-velocity algorithm. **Strongest evidence:** the Hades reference class is the closest-fit positive precedent in the corpus and the swarm did not test the current pivot against it. **DA steel-man caveat (Section B-2):** paid EA is itself a structural mid-dev pivot under Agent 6's taxonomy, and Finding 1-06-9 ("underbaked launches leave no recovery path") cuts harder against paid EA than free launch — the moment paid-EA reviews go mixed, recovery is harder.

### Alternative 2 — Single-product premium launch at $15-20 (Shape A)

**The "$15-20 + quality exceeds expectations" finding from 2025 Steam data is the most underweighted signal in the current pivot.** Hollow Knight Silksong $20 → $81.6M; Clair Obscur $49.99 → 4.6M units 95% positive; Balatro $14.99 → multi-million units (Finding 1-10-3). Under-$10 conversion is ~15% and $10+ conversion is ~10% — meaning a $15-20 paid product converts wishlists to dollars nearly as efficiently as a $9.99 product converts them to installs. Free, by contrast, has *no conversion event* until the successor MtM exists. The two-product architecture exists largely to dodge the premium-launch decision; if AtoA is good enough to ship, charging $15 for it skips the entire MtM-as-monetization-recovery dependency. **Strongest evidence:** Steam algorithm rewards momentum signal (review velocity, wishlist conversion), not price point — paid signal beats free signal at equivalent quality. **Caveat:** premium with unknown IP and seven-mode-type promise is a quality bar at Clair-Obscur level; below that bar, total flop with no on-ramp.

### Alternative 3 — Hybrid premium + permanently-free benchmark mode (Shape K)

**Splits the lobby/sandbox/customization layer permanently free as the discovery engine, while gating the campaign + commander progression + ranked behind a single premium purchase.** This collapses the sequential two-product split into one product with two surfaces: a free perpetual lobby/custom-mode sandbox (the SC1/WC3-recognizability promise lives here, available to anyone) and a paid campaign-plus-progression product. Promise-2 fit is a 5/5 because it directly funds the lobby-as-discovery-engine without requiring it to monetize itself. Promise-1 fit is 4/5 because the seven mode-types can be split meaningfully across the firewall. Promise-3 fit is 3/5 — BP/Ranked layer cleanly onto the paid side without compromising the free side. **Strongest evidence:** the BTD6 model, Deep Rock Galactic's free seasons + paid base, and Path of Exile's free-to-play-with-paid-cosmetics split are working analogs; none have been applied to TW specifically. **Caveat:** firewall design is hard, and the free side risks cannibalizing the paid side without careful scope discipline.

## DA Reckoning (mandatory accept-or-rebut for each challenge)

**1. The pivot-self-indictment (Agent 6 says mid-dev pivots are top-3 killer; the current pivot is itself a mid-dev pivot; Agent 10's paid-EA nomination is another pivot).** **PARTIALLY ACCEPT.** Agent 6's finding overgeneralizes the word "pivot" — the dead games (OMD!U MOBA→co-op, DD2 MOBA→TD, Defenders-of-Ardania genre identity drift) pivoted *what game they were* mid-development, abandoning audience commitments. The HTML-prototype → engine-product transition is a *substrate* pivot, not an *identity* pivot — the design constraints, mode coverage, and audience promise are continuous across both sides. However, the steel-man bites: any monetization shape change post-prototype carries the same goodwill-breach risk that killed DD2. The reckoning is not "Agent 6 is wrong" but "the pivot-as-killer finding applies to identity pivots; AtoA's substrate pivot is exposed only if the audience commitments shift, and Promise 1+2+3 stability across the substrate boundary is the load-bearing variable." Confidence on the original pivot framing: downgraded from "obviously fine" to "fine if and only if audience commitments stay stable across the boundary."

**2. The shared-source convergence on SC1/WC3 lobby gap.** **ACCEPT.** Five agents converging on three sources is source-amplification. The lobby gap is real but the *audience-size* implied by the convergence is overstated. Strength downgrade applied: convergence treated as "discourse-dominant signal" rather than "five-source triangulation." R2 framing should test gap-size, not gap-existence.

**3. The reconciled HTML-prototype-preserved number.** **ACCEPT.** Agent 1's 65% conflated stubs-as-preserved with deliverable-as-preserved. Reconciled honest figure: ~50-55% deliverable-logic preserved + ~10-15% spec-blocked + ~30-35% rebuild. See dedicated reconciliation section above.

**4. The Stormgate-AtoA resource asymmetry ($36M ex-Blizzard vs solo).** **ACCEPT (downgrade Promise-1 confidence).** Stormgate had funding and veterans and shipped incomplete. AtoA's solo-dev posture cannot credibly claim a higher polish-bar by force of will. What AtoA has that Stormgate did not: tight scope discipline (single design vision), no publisher-pressure pivots, no team-conflict resolution overhead, and the ability to ship-when-ready not ship-when-marketing-window-demands. Whether those structural advantages overcome the resource gap is a genuinely open question worth elevating in R2. Confidence on "polish-complete coverage of seven mode-types at solo scope": downgraded from implicit-high to "load-bearing assumption requiring explicit defense."

**5. The "BTD6 wins on breadth" generalization.** **PARTIALLY ACCEPT.** BTD6's 13-year-IP-equity confound is real (DA Section A-1). Citing BTD6's breadth as evidence that AtoA's 4x-greater scope-breadth wins is a generalization the swarm did not earn. However, the *negative* version of the lesson holds — Iron Marines' three-tower-types is a documented anti-pattern, Defenders-of-Ardania thinness is a documented kill — so the breadth direction is supported even if the BTD6-specific magnitude is not. R2 framing should rebuild the breadth-wins case from non-BTD6 evidence.

**6. Vocal-minority vs silent-majority calibration of Player Complaint Atlas.** **ACCEPT.** Atlas treated as worst-case-flagging (warranted), not as risk-weighted design driver (unwarranted without silent-majority data). Promise-3 inferences derived from the atlas downgraded accordingly.

**7. Agent-4-vs-Agent-9 creator-coverage contradiction.** **ACCEPT as unresolved tension.** Cannot have both "creators have departed" and "creators await novelty." The unresolved question worth asking is whether AtoA's creator-tooling investment is funding a creator scene that returns, or building features that find a different discovery path. Treat creator-tooling as a *bet*, not a known-good lever.

**8. Promise-3-as-anti-pattern.** **REBUT in part.** Findings 1-08-23 and 1-06-1 do disagree, but the disagreement resolves: 1-06-1 attacks F2P/cosmetics/MTX models grafted onto co-op/PvE; 1-08-23 attacks any monetization introduced *after* a free baseline. Both are consistent with the position "BP visible from day one + paid base + cosmetic-only constraint" — the model 1-06-1 attacks (F2P live-service) is not the model AtoA proposes (paid-base with optional BP layer). Confidence on Promise-3-as-shippable: medium-high *if and only if* the model never shifts toward F2P-cosmetics-live-service post-launch.

**9. Promise-2 substrate-cost exposure (Blizzard-subsidized historical precedent).** **ACCEPT.** Lobby-substrate cost is the unsolved economics piece Agent 6 surfaced and the swarm did not answer. Steam Lobby APIs piggyback some hosting cost, P2P-host-driven matches eliminate central-server cost, and Promise-2's customizability does not strictly require persistent server-side state — but the funding mechanism for long-tail moderation, hostbot-equivalent infrastructure, and creator IP stewardship is unspecified. **10. The vocal-minority attack on Wave-1 broadly.** **ACCEPT.** Most Wave-1 findings are negative-corpus-derived. The DA's framing — that Wave 1 sampled on the dependent variable for failure-pattern claims — is correct. Findings reframed accordingly: failure-pattern claims = high confidence; risk-weighted design recommendations derived from failure patterns = medium confidence absent silent-majority calibration.

**11. The Stormgate / AtoA divergence question.** **PARTIALLY REBUT.** The pivot's logic differs from Stormgate's in three ways the swarm did surface: (a) Stormgate shipped incomplete *modes* of an RTS game; AtoA's pivot is to ship complete *seven mode-types*, which is the explicit immune response — Stormgate's lesson supports Promise 1, not against it; (b) Stormgate priced-up cosmetics at launch ($12.99 vs SC2's $6.25), AtoA's cosmetic-only-no-pay-to-win commitment differs structurally; (c) Stormgate had publisher / VC pressure to ship on a fundraising window; solo AtoA does not. **12. SC1/WC3 nostalgia-cohort age question (DA A-6 / A-11).** **PARTIALLY ACCEPT.** The 32-42-year-old-with-90-minutes-weekly demographic argument is qualitatively correct but quantitatively unsupported in the DA pass. The reachable WC3-nostalgic audience may be smaller than the rhetorical mass; it may also be larger than the DA implies (Legion TD 2's 800 daily concurrent is a non-zero floor, and the SC2 Squadron TD orphan audience exists). Treat the addressable-audience-size question as the unresolved variable R2 should test directly.
