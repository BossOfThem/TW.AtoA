## Framing

Reading the ten Wave-1 outputs adversarially, the swarm exhibits three distinct epistemic vulnerabilities. First, a five-agent convergence on the "SC1/WC3 lobby gap" (agents 2/3/4/5/9) that on inspection cites a remarkably small substrate of shared sources — Hive Workshop threads, the same 2-3 PC Gamer / Massively OP articles, identical Legion-TD-2 dev interviews — and risks shared-echo rather than independent triangulation. Second, a near-total dependence on negative-leaning corpora (Reddit-negative-reviews, postmortems of the dead, creators-who-quit, complaint atlases) without an equally rigorous attempt to find the silent-majority signal — the swarm is sampling on the dependent variable for failure-pattern claims and on the vocal-minority for sentiment claims. Third, an uneasy contradiction between Agent 6's "underbaked launches die / mid-dev pivots are top-3 killer" thesis and Agent 10's "paid Early Access outperforms free EA" recommendation, which is itself a structural mid-dev pivot away from the sequential free-then-paid plan. The synthesizer cannot bury this; the entire pivot's credibility hinges on the reconciliation. The DA pass below surfaces 12 counter-arguments, 6 steel-mans, 7 epistemic attacks, and 9 contradictions, then names a required reckoning the synthesizer must address explicitly.

## Section A — Counter-arguments to high-strength / high-confidence findings

1. **Target: FINDING-1-02-1 (BTD6 sustains 97% via mode-variety).** Counter: BTD6's sentiment ceiling is at least equally explained by Ninja Kiwi's IP equity carried across BTD1–BTD5 over 13 years. A first-time IP cannot inherit that goodwill. The "mode-variety wins" reading conflates a launch attribute with a long-tail attribute layered over five prior titles. **Severity: high.** This finding is the single most direct external validation of Promise 1; if it's actually a long-tail-IP-equity story, Promise 1's evidence base shrinks materially.

2. **Target: FINDING-1-02-2 (WC3 customs unreplicated; Promise 2 unmet wish).** Counter: "Players keep returning to Reforged" is reported sentiment, not behavior data. Reforged's actual concurrent has collapsed; the people *talking about it* online are the residue, not a market. The "unmet wish" frame may describe a rhetorical position, not a paying audience. **Severity: high.**

3. **Target: FINDING-1-02-3 (Legion TD 2 = 800 daily concurrent proves underserved niche).** Counter: 800 concurrent daily is a *floor for survival*, not a *ceiling for an addressable market*. AtoA's pivot needs to clear ten times that to fund 7 mode-types. The same datapoint Wave-1 reads as proof-of-demand is also evidence the niche is too small to sustain the ambitious scope. **Severity: high.**

4. **Target: FINDING-1-03-5 (Legion TD 2 devs credit WC3 editor accessibility).** Counter: A successful dev's stated retrospection on why their game works is among the most contaminated possible signal — survivorship bias plus self-narrative bias. The WC3 editor was many things; the devs picked one. The same devs would credit something else if their game had failed. **Severity: medium.**

5. **Target: FINDING-1-03-1 (Sanctum 2 cut RPG/mazing to focus core).** Counter: Sanctum 2's commercial outcome was modest (37 CCU long-tail, no Sanctum 3). Reading Sanctum 2's cut-list as best-practice is reading lessons from a game that **didn't actually win**. The lesson "cut to focus" may have been wrong; perhaps Sanctum 2 needed *more* mode coverage, not less. **Severity: medium.**

6. **Target: FINDING-1-04-2 (creators name WC3 lobby as never-replicated golden age).** Counter: Creator nostalgia for WC3 customs is dated 2002–2008 — content authored when the audience was 14–22 years old. That cohort is now 32–42 with kids and 90 minutes a week. The phenomenon is real; the reachable audience that wants to relive it on Steam in 2026 may be a fraction of the rhetorical mass. **Severity: high.**

7. **Target: FINDING-1-04-4 (BTD6 wins on content surface, not mechanical reinvention).** Counter: BTD6 is a single-aesthetic, single-faction, mobile-derived product. Citing it as evidence that 7-mode-type / 3-civ / 9-Fusion-God / 3-commander breadth wins is generalizing across a 4x scope multiplier. The surface-area math doesn't transfer linearly. **Severity: high.**

8. **Target: FINDING-1-05-08 (Defenders of Ardania = cautionary precedent).** Counter: Defenders of Ardania failed for execution-quality reasons (described as "awkward", broken balance) — generalizing to "the WC3-PvP-TW shape is risky" reads execution failure as category failure. A single bad execution is not a category indictment. **Severity: medium.**

9. **Target: FINDING-1-06-2 (mid-dev pivots are top-3 cause of TW death).** Counter: AtoA's *own* current pivot (HTML free → premium MtM successor) is itself a mid-dev pivot. The finding's logic, applied to AtoA, is self-indicting — yet Wave-1 doesn't reckon with this. Either the finding overgeneralizes "pivot" or AtoA is exposed to its own diagnosis. **Severity: very high.** This is the single most damaging cross-finding indictment in the swarm.

10. **Target: FINDING-1-08-3 (Stormgate launched too early — strongest cautionary precedent for Promise 1).** Counter: Stormgate had $36M+ funding and a team of ex-Blizzard veterans and *still* shipped an incomplete state. Citing this as "AtoA must ship complete" is implicitly assuming a solo dev can clear a quality bar that a $36M studio missed. The finding may be evidence that the bar is unreachable at AtoA's resource level. **Severity: very high.**

11. **Target: FINDING-1-09-1 (custom-lobby pipeline is the most-mourned missing feature).** Counter: "Most-mourned by creators" is a function of who's left talking. Players who never cared about custom lobbies aren't on Hive Workshop in 2026; they're on Steam playing BTD6 or aren't here at all. The mourning-curve is a self-selected sample of WC3 die-hards. **Severity: high.**

12. **Target: FINDING-1-10-1 (paid EA outperforms free EA on engagement metrics).** Counter: The cited 38.9h vs 27.1h playtime gap is selection-confounded — paid EA filters for high-intent buyers who would over-perform on any engagement metric. This isn't evidence paid EA *causes* better engagement; it's evidence paid EA *selects* better-engaging audiences. The "EA conversion is built in" argument doesn't actually solve AtoA's discoverability problem if the audience can't find the EA listing. **Severity: high.**

## Section B — Steel-mans of opposing positions

1. **Steel-man against pursuing the SC1/WC3 lobby gap (vs. agents 2/3/4/5/9 convergence).** The lobby gap is unfilled because filling it is an unsolved economics problem: WC3 customs ran on Blizzard-subsidized servers, unpaid creator labor, and a population pool of millions playing a $50 RTS the customs piggybacked on. None of those preconditions exist for a standalone solo title. Agent 6 itself says "WC3 customs worked because Blizzard ate the host cost" (FINDING-1-06-8). The gap may not be "unmet demand" — it may be "structurally impossible at solo-shippable scope." Pursuing it is chasing a phantom whose absence is informative.

2. **Steel-man against Agent 10's paid-EA recommendation.** Paid EA is a structural commitment to ship a 1.0 within ~18 months under public review velocity scrutiny, with refund-rage if the velocity stalls. AtoA's solo-dev resource profile makes that velocity commitment a higher-variance bet than free-launch, which has no review-velocity floor. Agent 6's FINDING-1-06-9 ("underbaked launches leave no recovery path") cuts directly *against* paid EA: the moment paid-EA reviews go mixed, recovery is harder than recovery from a free launch where expectations are lower.

3. **Steel-man against the seven-mode-types-at-launch promise (vs. Promise 1).** A focused two-mode-type launch that nails Tower + PVP-Co-op (the two with strongest existing demand evidence) and *publicly defers* Hero/Horde/Custom/Campaign/Line to post-launch chapters is closer to the Hades / Vampire Survivors / BTD6 success template. They each shipped narrow and grew. Promise 1's all-modes-at-launch may be the trap, not the differentiator.

4. **Steel-man against Promise 3's Battle Pass at launch.** Agents 2/5/6/8 all flag BP as risk. The strongest case for *no Battle Pass at all* (vs. "carefully designed BP") is that any BP is a long-tail liability the solo dev cannot service over years; commitment to a launch BP is commitment to a season-2 BP, which is a commitment the solo dev cannot credibly make. "No BP" is cleaner than "BP done right."

5. **Steel-man against the "free HTML AtoA generates audience for premium MtM" thesis.** The two-product split assumes AtoA's free-product audience converts to MtM purchases. The actual conversion rate from free-Steam to paid-sequel is empirically near-zero outside of major publisher franchises. AtoA-as-free may consume the launch attention budget without delivering wishlist transfer to MtM; one product priced correctly is strictly better than two products with broken hand-off.

6. **Steel-man against the "creator-friendly tooling differentiates" claim (FINDING-1-09-5/8).** Creator tooling is a feature whose ROI is measured in creator-driven discovery, but creators *don't cover TW* (FINDING-1-09-6). Building features for an audience that has structurally departed the genre is sunk cost. The right move may be ignoring creators entirely and building for the silent-player-majority that doesn't watch streams.

## Section C — Epistemic attacks on load-bearing findings

1. **Vocal-minority attack on Agent 8's Player Complaint Atlas (24 findings).** Steam negative-reviews are by definition the angry. Forum complaint threads are the engaged-and-unhappy. Reddit posts are the literate-and-motivated. None of these sample the silent majority of TW players who installed, played 8 hours, churned, and never spoke. Agent 8's atlas is empirically a *complaint distribution among complainants*, not a *risk distribution among players*. Severity: this affects nearly every Promise-3 inference Wave 1 makes.

2. **Survivorship bias in Agent 3's GDC postmortems.** Only studios that survived (or that died publicly) write postmortems. Sanctum 2 wrote one; the dozens of TW games that died silently did not. The "common lessons" in the postmortem corpus are the lessons of the *interesting* deaths, not the *typical* deaths. The actual modal failure mode of TW games is obscurity, not pivots — and obscurity doesn't generate postmortems.

3. **Sampling-on-the-dependent-variable in Agent 6's tombstones.** Every dead TW game in retrospect "looks like" the other dead TW games — researcher pattern-matching produces taxonomies whether or not the patterns predict. Without a control group of *living* TWs cross-checked for the same anti-patterns, "monetization-mode mismatch" and "scope-pivot" are descriptive, not causal. BTD6 has had monetization shifts; it's alive. Why?

4. **Author-as-auditor optimism bias (Agent 1 HTML audit) vs. pessimism bias (Agent 7 HTML bias check).** Agent 1 reports 65% preserved, low/medium regression for most features. Agent 7 reports fusion stubbed, lineages obsoleted, targeting unspecified, cosmetics dead-end. These are the *same prototype* read by two roles with opposite incentives. Neither is calibrated against an external port-cost benchmark. The reconciled honest number is unknown and possibly closer to Agent 7's read than Agent 1's headline.

5. **Outcome bias in Agent 5's competitor teardowns.** Wave 1 knows BTD6 succeeded and Defenders of Ardania failed. The teardowns retrofit explanations onto known outcomes. Defenders of Ardania's "no lobby mutators" is named as cause, but a hundred TWs without lobby mutators succeeded. Outcome-known reasoning is the weakest form of pattern inference.

6. **Convergence-as-echo on the "SC1/WC3 lobby gap" (agents 2/3/4/5/9).** Five agents agree. On inspection, they cite overlapping sources: Hive Workshop, PC Gamer's Legion-TD-2 interview, Massively OP's Stormgate coverage, the same "Blizzard Killed Custom Maps" forum thread. Five agents reading three sources is not five-source triangulation; it's source-amplification. The convergence may not be empirical — it may be the swarm picking up the same loudest 3-4 essays that frame the discourse.

7. **Recency bias in Agent 9's creator complaints.** Stormgate (2024) and Reforged (2020) dominate the corpus. Pre-Reforged creator sentiment toward the genre is barely sampled. The "creators have given up on TW" claim may be a 4-year window mistaken for a generational shift.

8. **Definitional drift in Agent 10's "paid EA outperforms free EA."** The 24% / 23.67% revenue figures aggregate paid EA across all genres. TW-specific paid-EA outcomes are not isolated. Generalizing genre-blind metrics to the TW niche is unsupported by the cited data.

9. **Self-fulfilling FINDING-1-08-23 (monetization-anger keyed to change).** This is presented as a synthesis across PvZ2/BTD6/Underlords. But the three cases are all post-launch monetization additions. A counterfactual case (game with monetization on day 1 that still got hated) isn't sampled. The "anger keyed to change, not existence" claim cannot be tested without that counterfactual.

10. **Source-availability bias in Agent 4's longform creator essays.** GMTK / Dissecting Design / CritPoints are available because they're the best-known. Their TD coverage is a few essays in a much larger oeuvre. The claim that creators "converge on solved-meta" rests on perhaps four essays across three creators, none TW-focused. This is a thin substrate.

## Section D — Cross-finding contradictions

1. **Agent 1 (65% preserved) vs. Agent 7 (fusion stubbed, cosmetics dead-end, targeting unspecified, lineages obsoleted).** Same prototype, two readings. Agent 1's "feature-complete for its scope" frames the codebase as a successful design-validation tool; Agent 7 frames it as having endgame-as-concept-not-delivery. The honest reconciled number is materially below 65%; the synthesizer must commit to a reconciled figure or acknowledge unknowability.

2. **Agent 6's FINDING-1-06-2 ("mid-dev pivot top-3 killer") vs. Agent 10's nomination of paid-EA pivot.** Paid EA from a sequential-free starting point is itself a mid-dev structural pivot in monetization, distribution, and audience promise. The two findings cannot both be right unmodified; either pivot-as-killer overgeneralizes, or paid-EA-nomination understates pivot risk.

3. **Agent 4's FINDING-1-04-1 ("design-locked TD gets free narrative tailwind for novelty") vs. Agent 6's FINDING-1-06-9 ("underbaked launches die").** Novelty is rewarded; incompleteness is punished. AtoA's 7-mode promise tries to be both novel (breadth) and complete (every mode polish-grade). Agent 6 says completeness is what sells; Agent 4 says novelty is what gets covered. These pull in different launch postures.

4. **Agent 8's FINDING-1-08-23 (monetization-anger keyed to *change*) vs. Agent 6's FINDING-1-06-1 (F2P-cosmetics monetization fails when grafted onto co-op/PvE).** Agent 8 says the model can be fine if it's there from day one. Agent 6 says the model itself is structurally wrong for the game shape regardless of timing. These are genuinely different findings and the synthesizer must pick one.

5. **Agent 2's FINDING-1-02-3 (Legion TD 2 = 800 daily concurrent = proof of demand) vs. Agent 6's tombstone for Tower Wars (2012) Steam (71% positive, dead matchmaking pool).** Two PvP-TWs with positive critical reception; one survives at small concurrent, one died. The variable separating them is unidentified by either agent. Without that variable, both findings are decorative.

6. **Agent 5's FINDING-1-05-12 (SC2 Squadron TD orphan audience portable) vs. Agent 4's FINDING-1-04-3 (SC2 Arcade structurally failed; players didn't show up despite editor power).** If SC2 Arcade structurally failed at audience-formation with Blizzard's distribution, why does an SC2 Squadron TD orphan audience exist to be ported? The two findings can both be true only if the orphan is much smaller than implied.

7. **Agent 3's FINDING-1-03-5 (Legion TD 2 devs credit accessibility, not power) vs. Agent 5's FINDING-1-05-12 (Squadron TD = highest-skill-ceiling = orphan audience).** Accessibility and skill-ceiling are different design goals. AtoA cannot inherit both reference classes. Which one is the target?

8. **Agent 8's FINDING-1-08-14 (RTS onboarding broken; campaign-as-tutorial is the answer) vs. Agent 5's FINDING-1-05-14 (Kingdom Rush hand-crafted campaign incompatible with procedural lobby chaos).** Agent 8 wants Promise 1's PVE-Campaign to do tutorial duty; Agent 5 says hand-crafted campaign and lobby modes are different products glued at the spine. Treating Campaign as the onboarding-by-stealth lane and as a polish-complete mode-type and as a tutorial is asking one mode to do three jobs.

9. **Agent 9's FINDING-1-09-6 (creator coverage of TW underperforms structurally — audience economics debuff) vs. Agent 4's FINDING-1-04-1 + FINDING-1-09-10 (creator-narrative tailwind awaits novel TW).** These cannot both be operative. Either creators have departed the genre and won't return for novelty (Agent 9), or creators are awaiting novelty to return (Agent 4 / 9-10). Wave 1 endorses both without reconciling.
