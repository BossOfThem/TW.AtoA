## 1. Q1 — Scope-cut precondition

The Promise-1 swarmer's reachability verdict — that no surveyed solo-dev shipper has ever delivered seven mode-types feature-complete, that the closest "add one mode-type post-EA" event ran 18 small-team-months at compromised quality, and that the $40M ceiling reference (Stormgate) failed at exactly AtoA's mode-type spread — is the load-bearing finding underneath this question. Both R2 self-scores of Promise-1 at 4/5 are demonstrably unearned. The question now is whether the project responds by editing the published commitment (Response A) or by holding the commitment and routing scope discipline through paid-EA's externalized refund-backed roadmap (Response B). Both responses are coherent under different theories of how product discipline actually operates at solo scale.

### Cycle 1 — Response A pro (edit the north-star)

The strongest case for Response A is that an unedited Promise 1 is a guaranteed broken promise on the public storefront from day one of any commercial event, and broken promises are the dominant anger-vector the entire R1 corpus identified (FINDING-1-08-23, Player Complaint Atlas Bucket 5, Stormgate as the canonical 2024 case). Holding seven mode-types in writing while the swarm has produced strong evidence the promise cannot be honored is structurally identical to Stormgate's pre-launch RTS feature-list — a marketing artifact that survives until first-contact and then becomes the indictment. The honest move is to pre-empt the indictment by editing the artifact: ship Tower + PvP-Co-op feature-complete at 1.0 (the two mode-types with the strongest existing demand evidence per Player-lens reference-class match to Legion TD 2 and per the densest R1 convergence), with Hero / Horde / Custom / PVE-Campaign / Line as either roadmap-deferred or written-off, captured in a dated `decisions/` entry under cascade discipline. Response A's deeper virtue is that it converts the path debate from decorative back into substantive: at cut-scope Promise 1, both paths score meaningfully on Promise-1 reachability rather than both scoring 2/5 against an unreachable bar. The PM-lens favoritism for paid-EA on cash-flow / scope-discipline / substrate-funding becomes actionable rather than moot; the Player-lens's six convergent player-felt asymmetries land on a product that can be shipped. Response A also operationalizes the cascade-discipline principle in `CLAUDE.md`: a Phase-N decision (R3's reachability finding) that requires a Phase-N-1 edit (the north-star scope) goes back upstream properly and gets a written override — exactly the mechanism the project's own discipline rules describe. Refusing to edit when the evidence demands editing is the silent scope-creep the discipline exists to prevent, just inverted in valence.

### Cycle 2 — Response B pro (hold the commitment, route through paid-EA externalized discipline)

The strongest case for Response B is that scope cuts decided before player contact are systematically worse than scope cuts forced by player contact, because pre-contact cuts are made by a dev whose model of what the audience wants is the same model that produced the over-scoped commitment in the first place. Editing Promise 1 to "Tower + PvP-Co-op" right now is a bet that those are the right two mode-types — a bet made by the same judgment that previously bet on seven. Paid-EA's externalized scope-discipline mechanism (PM-lens FINDING-3PM-5: public refund-backed roadmap forces audience-justified scope cuts) is structurally a *better* scope-cutting instrument than private decision-folder edits, because the cut is made against revealed audience priorities rather than predicted ones. Caves of Qud's nine-year EA arc is the positive reference: the team held an ambitious commitment, let revenue and player engagement determine which features actually got engineered, cut piecewise in public, and shipped 1.0 with a substrate that no pre-cut plan would have generated. Response B's deeper virtue is that it preserves the marketing surface — the seven-mode commitment is itself the differentiator the R1 corpus found vacant in the standalone TW market (Findings 1-04-5, 1-04-6, 1-09-10), and pre-emptively cutting it surrenders the differentiation without testing whether the audience would in fact reward visible incremental delivery against the full commitment. Players punish *reversal-of-promise* (FINDING-1-08-23), but paid-EA's frame is "this is a partial game I am iterating publicly toward seven modes, you are paying $15 to come along" — incremental delivery against a public commitment is honored under EA convention even when the commitment is later trimmed, because the trim is itself negotiated in public with paying customers. The asymmetric-development flag in alignment-check-3 explicitly named Response B as under-developed in R3, and the case is real: paid-EA is the only product-shape that natively expresses "hold the commitment, let revealed engagement drive cuts."

### Cycle 3 — Converged answer + residual uncertainty

**Response A wins the precondition role; Response B wins the operational mechanism.** The two responses are not a binary fork — they are sequential. The honest read of the swarm is that Promise 1 at frozen seven-mode scope is unreachable on either path within a viable indie timeframe (Promise-1 swarmer's verdict, undisputed across R3 outputs), and refusing to edit the published commitment in advance of first contact is a bet that the externalized refund-backed roadmap can absorb a public cut from seven to two-or-three without reading as Stormgate-class reversal. That bet is structurally available under paid-EA but is not free: every successful piecewise-cut-during-EA case in the searched evidence base (Caves of Qud, Hades depth-during-EA, Slay the Spire incremental characters) cut *depth-against-a-single-mode-type*, not *breadth-across-mode-types*. The Promise-1 swarmer's Finding 10 is explicit that no precedent supports adding mode-types during EA; Response B asks the inverse question (cutting mode-types during EA against a written commitment to seven) which is also evidentially empty. The converged answer is therefore: **Response A is the precondition** — a written, dated `decisions/` entry must edit Promise 1 to a smaller initial-coverage commitment (most defensibly Tower + PvP-Co-op feature-complete at 1.0, with the remaining five mode-types framed as roadmap stretch or post-1.0 chapters) before either path can be honestly recommended. **Response B's operational mechanism is then layered on top under paid-EA shape**: the cut commitment is held publicly via refund-backed roadmap, and any further trims happen in public against revealed audience engagement rather than dev guesswork. This composition resolves the alignment-check-3 MEDIUM flag (Response B is now developed symmetrically) while honoring the cascade discipline that Response A enforces. The PM does not have to pick *between* A and B; they pick A *as the precondition* and B *as the operating mechanism*.

Residual uncertainty: the specific mode-type cut (Tower + PvP-Co-op is the strongest pair on R1+R3 convergence but not the only defensible pair — Tower + Custom is also coherent given Promise 2's lobby-substrate weight, and Player-lens FINDING-3PL-2's reference-class match to Legion TD 2 specifically nominates PvP-Co-op rather than Custom). The cut also interacts with the 3-civ × 9-Fusion-God × 3-commander multipliers, which the Promise-1 swarmer flagged but did not isolate; trimming mode-types may not be sufficient if the per-mode multipliers themselves carry unbenchmarked solo-scope cost. Cycle 1 cannot resolve this and will leave it for Cycle 2 / synthesis.

## 2. Q2 — WC3→Dota2 pattern weight

The pivot's only structural advantage in R3 is the Promise-2 swarmer's Finding 10: the WC3→Dota2 lineage is the single best-documented case of a free custom-lobby substrate generating downstream commercial value, and the pivot is the only proposed shape that explicitly enacts the upstream/downstream pattern. The Player-lens findings (3PL-9 price-anchor reset, 3PL-7 beta-tester perception) attack the pattern's player-felt translation. The question is whether the lineage argument is load-bearing enough to override the convergent PM-lens / Player-lens preference for paid-EA, given that every historically successful enactment had a platform-giant funder rather than a solo dev playing both ends.

### Cycle 1 — pattern-survives-at-scale pro

The strongest case that the pattern survives even at solo scale is that the *causal mechanism* of the WC3→Dota2 lineage is not the platform-giant's funding — it is the validation-cheap-substrate-then-engineered-successor *architecture*, and that architecture is portable to any resource scale that can fund both ends. Blizzard's role in WC3 was not "spend lots of money on the substrate"; it was "have the substrate happen to exist as a side-effect of shipping a $50 RTS." Valve's role in Dota 2 was not "fund the lineage"; it was "recognize a validated game-shape and engineer the polished version." The pattern's load-bearing variable is *substrate-cheapness combined with successor-distinctness*, not *funder-magnitude*. A solo dev playing both ends can replicate the architecture if AtoA-free's substrate is genuinely cheap (HTML, Steam Lobby APIs, P2P, no central state — Arguer A FINDING-2A-5) and MtM is genuinely a different engineering investment (engine-grade, paid). The Auto Chess → TFT/Underlords lineage actually strengthens this reading: Drodo was a small Chinese studio, not a platform giant, and the validated mod successfully spawned multiple commercial successors across studios. The pattern works at studio scale; the question of whether it works at solo scale is empirically open but not foreclosed by the platform-giant cases. Promise-2 swarmer Finding 10's own framing is that the substrate's *value* is realized by spawning successors — the dev's job is to ship the substrate cheaply enough that the successor can afford to finish it, which is precisely what Arguer A's two-shot operationalization names. The Player-lens objections (price-anchor reset, beta-tester perception) are real but conditional on framing: Vampire Survivors maintained free-itch.io and paid-Steam without the contradiction collapsing the franchise, which Player-lens FINDING-3PL-1 itself concedes. The pattern is portable; the question is execution discipline, not structural availability.

### Cycle 2 — pattern-dies-without-platform-giant pro

The strongest counter is that every successful enactment of the lineage in the evidence base had two specifically-platform-giant resources that solo-dev cannot replicate, and these resources are not optional decorations of the pattern — they are the load-bearing substrate the pattern actually runs on. First, the upstream funder ate the substrate's marginal cost at zero return because the substrate was a side-effect of an already-successful $50 RTS with millions of players generating server-load that justified the infrastructure independently. AtoA-free has no such pre-existing audience to amortize against; the substrate's full cost falls on a free product with no revenue, and the "cheap substrate" claim assumes Steam Lobby APIs and P2P are sufficient — which Promise-2 swarmer's five-tier decomposition explicitly contradicts (the data/scripting layer, Tier 2, is the actual ceiling, and Steam Lobby APIs do not provide it). Second, the downstream successor was funded by a platform giant that hired the substrate's creator (Valve hired IceFrog) or built a parallel commercial product on validated mechanics (Riot built TFT) — in both cases the funder had access to capital and engineering teams the solo dev does not. The Auto Chess → TFT case Cycle 1 invoked actually proves this asymmetry: Drodo *did not build TFT*; Riot did. Drodo built the mod that validated the game-shape; the commercial successor was built by a separate, well-funded entity. The pattern's solo-dev replication would require the dev to play *both* the substrate-builder role (free, validation-cheap) and the engineered-successor role (paid, polished, capital-intensive) — and the historical record is clean: nobody has done this at solo scale. The Player-lens findings (3PL-9, 3PL-7) cut sharper at solo scale than the platform-giant cases because price-anchor transfer is most violent when the two products are perceived as "the same dev's same game with better graphics" — which is exactly the pivot's "MtM inherits AtoA verbatim, only Phase 5 is fresh" framing (Arguer A concession b). The PM-lens FINDING-3PM-10 names this directly: the wishlist-conversion bet is the pivot's single point of failure with no positive solo-dev TW-genre evidence, and the WC3→Dota2 pattern's funder-asymmetry is the structural reason the bet is empty rather than incidentally untested.

### Cycle 3 — Converged answer + residual uncertainty

**The pattern survives at smaller scale only under two conditional strictures, neither of which the pivot currently honors; therefore the analogy does not in its current form override the convergent PM-lens / Player-lens preference for paid-EA.** The pattern's portable causal mechanism (validation-cheap-substrate-then-engineered-successor) is real and Cycle 1's argument is correct that funder-magnitude is not the strict load-bearing variable. But the historical record shows two structural conditions on portability: (1) the substrate must be genuinely cheap, which means accepting the Tier-2 (data/scripting) ceiling and shipping a thin substrate — terrain editor + workshop integration + replay only, not WC3-grade — and (2) the successor must be perceived as a *distinct* commercial product, not "the same game with better graphics," because the price-anchor-reset and beta-tester-perception costs (Player-lens 3PL-7, 3PL-9) compound when the two products are framed as continuous. The pivot doc's "MtM inherits AtoA verbatim, only Phase 5 is fresh" commitment violates condition (2) by construction; AtoA's seven-mode promise at the free tier violates condition (1) by demanding deep-substrate functionality the free product has no funding mechanism to ship. Under those violations, the WC3→Dota2 pattern reads as the *generous* analogy the pivot reaches for and the *unmatched* analogy the evidence supplies. The pattern survives at studio scale (Drodo→Riot) but not as currently structured at solo scale. The convergent PM-lens / Player-lens preference for paid-EA holds against the lineage argument because the lineage argument's preconditions are not met by the pivot as written; **paid-EA wins on the empirically-measurable axes the lenses read, and the historical-pattern argument does not have a load-bearing form available to the solo dev playing both ends.**

Residual uncertainty: a *re-architected* pivot that explicitly honors the two structural conditions — thin substrate at AtoA-free (terrain editor + workshop + replay, no data/scripting), genuine product-distinctness at MtM (different name, different art, different scope, no "same-game-better-graphics" framing) — could revive the lineage argument. This would be a meaningfully different pivot from the one decision-doc Loop-3 currently describes, and would require its own dated `decisions/` entry. Cycle 1 cannot adjudicate whether such a re-architecture is on the table; this is a PM-temperament and design-commitment question that R4 synthesis or PM directly will have to close. The Cycle-1 verdict is on the pivot *as currently structured*, not on every possible pivot.
