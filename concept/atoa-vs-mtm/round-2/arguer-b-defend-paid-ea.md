## 1. Steelman — paid-EA single-product

The strongest reading of the paid-EA shape is that it is **the only path that pays for itself while it is being built**, and that property dominates every other consideration for a solo dev with no audience. The current sequential pivot proposes to build a complete free product, ship it, build a complete second product on the engine, and ship that. Two products. Every commitment doubled. The bridge between them — wishlist conversion from a free Steam title to a paid sequel — is empirically near-zero outside major-publisher franchises (DA B-5, uncontested in R1). Paid EA replaces this bridge-of-faith with a single conversion event that happens at the moment of *first contact*: a player who finds AtoA on Steam either pays $15 or doesn't, and the dev knows immediately whether the product has product-market fit. There is no "we'll monetize the sequel" deferral. Funding follows shipping.

The reference class is the strongest in the entire R1 corpus and the swarm did not test the current pivot against it directly. Hades, Hades 2, Vampire Survivors, Manor Lords, They Are Billions, Slay the Spire, Darkest Dungeon, Dead Cells, Project Zomboid, Rimworld, Factorio — the unfinished-but-shippable strategy/action niche AtoA inhabits is *populated almost entirely by paid-EA-iterated titles*. Legion TD 2 is the direct genre comp and is itself a paid-EA-iterated single-product success. Stormgate's death (FINDING-1-10-2) is not a paid-EA cautionary tale — it is the *free*-EA cautionary tale. $36M and ex-Blizzard veterans shipped free EA and peaked under 1k concurrent. "Free for discoverability" did not solve the problem free is supposed to solve. The empirical record shows that Steam's algorithm rewards review velocity, wishlist conversion, and revenue momentum — *paid signal beats free signal at equivalent quality*, because the algorithm reads paid signal as commercial intent and free signal as noise.

The third pillar is honesty as a marketing surface. R1's deepest convergence in the Player Complaint Atlas (Bucket 5, FINDING-1-08-3, FINDING-1-08-23) is that players do not punish *incompleteness* — they punish *broken promises about completeness*. Stormgate promised a finished RTS and shipped EA. Underlords promised free progression and paywalled heroes. PvZ2 promised premium and retrofitted F2P. The unifying anger pattern is the *reversal*. Paid EA is the only shape in which "this is a partial game I am iterating on, and you are paying $15 to come along" is a *true statement on day one*, not a recovery from a betrayed earlier statement. The single product, the up-front honest framing, and the day-1-visible Battle Pass commitment (FINDING-1-08-23 cleanly satisfied) collectively constitute the *only* configuration of the three promises in which no audience commitment has to shift between launch and 1.0. Promise 1 is "we will reach polish-complete coverage of seven mode-types by 1.0, here is the roadmap, here is what you get today." Promise 2 is funded by paid revenue from day one rather than waiting for a sequel that may never ship. Promise 3 is paid-base + day-1-visible BP + cosmetic-only, which the DA Reckoning #8 already conceded resolves the 1-08-23 vs 1-06-1 contradiction cleanly.

## 3. Honest concessions — what paid-EA genuinely cannot defend

Paid-EA cannot defend against the **review-velocity guillotine**. FINDING-1-06-9 is a real attack: the moment paid-EA reviews go mixed, recovery is structurally harder than for a free launch, because refund rage compounds with discoverability collapse. A solo dev who ships paid EA with seven mode-types in alpha state and gets a 60% Steam review score is in a worse position than a solo dev whose free product simply has low concurrent. The current pivot's free shell is, in this specific sense, a softer landing pad — it cannot generate negative reviews that kill the product before it finds its audience.

Paid-EA also cannot defend against the **HTML sunk-cost reversal** as a real problem of work already done. The synthesizer's reconciled 50-55% of preserved logic is preserved across substrates, but the rendering, transport, and UI layer of the prototype — perhaps 30-35% of demonstrable session effort — is lost on engine pivot in a way it would not be lost on the current "ship HTML first" path. This is not a fatal argument (the design progress survives, the rendering is rebuilt fast in the engine) but it is honestly a real loss the current pivot avoids.

Paid-EA cannot defend against the **marketing-burden asymmetry**. Free-tier removes friction; the dev who has zero audience and no marketing budget genuinely faces a steeper week-1 hill in paid-EA. FINDING-2B-12 argues the gap is smaller than two-product believers assume, but smaller is not zero. A paid-EA launch with no wishlist and no creator coverage and no press hits will sell ~50 copies in week 1. The dev has to either accept that floor as the fitness signal it is, or pre-build wishlist through a year of devlog content before launch — neither is free.

Paid-EA cannot defend against the **creator-coverage bet**. R1 left the Agent-4 vs Agent-9 contradiction explicitly unresolved. Paid-EA's reference class (Hades, Vampire Survivors, Manor Lords) all benefited from creator coverage that found them; if the DA Section C-7 read is right and creators have departed the TW genre permanently, paid-EA inherits the discoverability problem with no compensating advantage from creator-funnel discovery. The bet remains a bet under any shape.

Paid-EA cannot defend against the **Stormgate-AtoA resource asymmetry** any more than the current pivot can. DA Reckoning #4 names this as the largest unexamined assumption in the swarm; paid-EA does not solve a solo dev's polish-bar question. It simply changes who pays during the polishing.

Every other shape requires either a free product (which exposes the day-1-visibility commitment to a shift at the paid handoff) or a model change at some boundary. Paid-EA is the configuration in which Promise 3's audience commitment is structurally identical at launch and at 1.0. Full marks.
