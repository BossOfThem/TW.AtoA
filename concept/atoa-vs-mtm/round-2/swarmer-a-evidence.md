## Honest negative results / hypotheses that didn't pan out

- **"Free-on-Steam beats paid in algorithmic discoverability."** Searched for direct evidence Steam's algorithm structurally favors free SKUs over paid. Did not find it. Valve has explicitly designed the algorithm around player-interest signals and rejected paid placement; F2P games benefit from engagement-based signals (CCU, retention) but there's no documented "free SKU gets a multiplier" mechanic. Arguer A should NOT claim algorithmic favoritism — only the lower-friction wishlist/install-claim funnel.
- **"Solo-dev free-then-premium two-product Steam case studies are common."** Could not surface a clean solo-dev exemplar where dev shipped Game A free on Steam specifically to seed Game B premium. Vampire Survivors (FINDING-1) is the closest, but the free version was on itch.io, not Steam, and the Steam release was the premium successor itself rather than a third product. The pivot pattern is more attested at studio scale (Drodo, Valve, Riot, tinyBuild) than at solo scale.
- **"Free demo of TW-genre game caused measurable premium-successor wishlist lift."** Could not find a TW-specific solo-dev case study with hard numbers. The Graveyard Keeper data (FINDING-6) is the closest analog but is not TW-genre and not solo-dev.

## Summary for Arguer A

Strongest pillars: FINDING-1 (Vampire Survivors prototype-to-premium), FINDING-2 (DotA→Dota 2), FINDING-3 (Auto Chess→TFT/Underlords), FINDING-4 (TD genre born in BW custom maps). These four together establish that the TW-adjacent genres AtoA targets have a documented historical pattern of free-validation-layer → premium-successor — and that the custom-lobby DNA (Promise 2) is exactly what travels through that funnel.

Funnel mechanics: FINDING-5 (prologue trick), FINDING-6 (tinyBuild numbers), FINDING-7 (Next Fest conversion) underwrite the discoverability premise quantitatively but with documented caveats (algorithm-change risk, wishlist≠sale, demo≠full-game).

Weakest leg: solo-dev replicability of the studio-scale precedents. Arguer A should expect Arguer B to attack here.
