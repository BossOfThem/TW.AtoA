# Agent 06 — Failed TW Tombstones

**Wave 1, research-only.** North-star verbatim:
> A solo-first, Steam-shippable game that delivers three promises at launch: (1) feature-, polish-, and art-complete coverage of every TW-genre mode-type — Tower / Line / Hero / Horde / Custom / PVE-Campaign / PVP-Co-op — with the full Ash to Altar scope (3 civs, full ladder, 9 Fusion Gods, 3 commanders); (2) the customizability and recognizability of SC1 / WC3 custom lobbies that no standalone TW has reproduced; (3) optional Battle Pass and Ranked Progression for grind-oriented players, layered without compromising regular play.

## What kills TW games — landscape

The morgue tells a remarkably consistent story. Tower Defense and Tower Wars titles rarely die from a single dramatic catastrophe; they die from **mode-and-monetization mismatch**, **scope-shape pivots that abandon their original audience**, and **always-online plumbing welded onto fundamentally single-player or small-multiplayer experiences**. The next-most-common cause is **competitive depth without lobby/community infrastructure** — games that copy the WC3 custom-map *form* without reproducing the host/browse/persist *substrate* that made those customs sticky. F2P + PvP-MOBA-shaped monetization grafted onto co-op or solo-friendly TW gameplay has a near-perfect death record. Critically, *none* of the dead games died because TW as a genre is unwanted; they died because they shipped a partial, mismatched, or service-dependent shape of it.

## Cause-of-death taxonomy

| Bucket | Root cause | Representative deaths |
|---|---|---|
| **A. Monetization-mode mismatch** | F2P/MTX model designed for PvP-MOBA grafted onto co-op/PvE TW | Orcs Must Die! Unchained, Dungeon Defenders 2 (early), Prime World Defenders |
| **B. Strategic pivot whiplash** | Project changes target genre/audience mid-development; loses both old and new audiences | OMD! Unchained (MOBA → co-op), Dungeon Defenders 2 (MOBA → TD), Defenders of Ardania (TD/RTS hybrid that satisfies neither) |
| **C. Server-rot / always-online debt** | Always-online dependencies on features that didn't need them; servers go dark, game loses leaderboards/achievements/progression | Prime World Defenders, Code Geass: Lost Stories, OMD! Unchained, Bloons Monkey City Flash |
| **D. Scope-collapse / underbaked launch** | Shipped without enough content, polish, or systems to sustain interest; no audience formed | Tower Wars (Steam, "no more content"), Anomaly Defenders (basic TD; "they decided to have some easy money") |
| **E. Crowdfunding / DOA** | Niche-hardcore TW couldn't reach mass-market funding floor | Always Outnumbered (5% of $175k Kickstarter goal) |
| **F. Live-service self-funding failure** | Operating costs > revenue; publisher can't justify keeping servers up | OMD! Unchained ("running at a financial loss"), Code Geass: Lost Stories |
| **G. Platform/tech rot** | Underlying platform deprecated (Flash), or engine sufficiently fragile that updates stop | Bloons Monkey City Flash (Flash EOL + NK login deprecation), various Flash-era TDs |
| **H. Lobby/community substrate absent** | Standalone TW copies WC3-custom *gameplay* without reproducing host/browse/lobby ecology, can't sustain multiplayer | Tower Wars (multiplayer matchmaking begging post on store page), most standalone Line TD attempts |
| **I. Promise-broken launches** | Devs reversed explicit promises (no MTX, no F2P, etc.); refund/rage cycles destroyed goodwill | Dungeon Defenders 2 (broke "no MTX" promise) |

## Per-game tombstones

**Orcs Must Die! Unchained (2017-2019).** Cause: A+B+F. Robot Entertainment built a F2P-cosmetics live-service economy designed for competitive PvP MOBA play, then pivoted away from PvP toward co-op TD when Siege mode failed (Siege players churned harder than non-Siege players). The cosmetics-driven F2P economy doesn't print money in co-op the way it does in MOBA-style social PvP. Ran at a loss for months; servers shut April 9, 2019. The cleanest "mode/monetization mismatch + pivot whiplash + can't self-fund" tombstone in the genre.

**Dungeon Defenders 2 (2014-2017+ EA).** Cause: B+I+A. Started life as a MOBA, scrapped that after community pushback, rebooted as F2P TD/action-RPG. Trendy explicitly promised no microtransactions and no F2P, then reversed both. Bag-slot/inventory MTX (~$60 to unlock baseline storage) interpreted as "blatant greed." Survived but never recovered the goodwill of DD1's audience.

**Prime World: Defenders (2013).** Cause: C+A. Single-player-flavoured collectible-card TD with always-online dependencies (leaderboards, achievements). Nival pulled servers without notice; "always-online" features broke for paying customers while devs migrated effort to a separate F2P MTX-heavy version. Bought game = bricked features.

**Tower Wars (Super Senior Games, 2012).** Cause: D+H. Steam release. Premise was solid (TW + RTS hybrid), but content shipped thin and never expanded ("No more content"). Multiplayer pool too small to self-sustain — current Steam page has pinned "post here if you want to find a match" thread. Demonstrates that **without a lobby/host substrate or steady content cadence, a multiplayer-first TW dies even with positive reviews** (71% positive, 1,201 reviews).

**Defenders of Ardania (Most Wanted Entertainment, 2012).** Cause: B+D. TD/RTS hybrid where neither half cohered — described as having interesting ideas but "awkward rather than organic" execution; concepts unexplained, balance broken. Hybrid genre attempts that fail to commit to either side are a recurring TW failure shape.

**Code Geass: Lost Stories (mobile, shut down 2024-08-29).** Cause: C+F. Licensed mobile TD strategy. Standard mobile-live-service decay: revenue couldn't justify running costs; downloads/purchases halted ~30 days before full shutdown.

**Bloons Monkey City Flash (Ninja Kiwi, ~2014-2025).** Cause: G+C. Ninja Kiwi's most ambitious city-builder/TD hybrid. Killed by Flash EOL + Ninja Kiwi's 2022 login deprecation + 2025 quiet server removal. The IP survived (BTD6 thrives); the *form factor* (Flash, account-tethered) died. Lesson: **online-account-tethering kills longevity even for popular IPs** when the platform substrate erodes.

**Always Outnumbered (Kickstarter, 2012).** Cause: E. Hardcore competitive TD. Reached ~5% of $175k goal. Developer post-mortem: hardcore-competitive TD lacks the mass appeal of PvZ / Desktop TD. Niche-hardcore TW positioning struggles to clear mass-market funding bars.

**Anomaly Defenders (11 bit, 2014).** Cause: D. The Anomaly franchise's distinctive identity was *tower offense* (you're the attacker). Switching to *traditional* TD for the finale shipped a "basic, traditional tower defense" with no differentiation. The series ended on this title. Lesson: **abandoning your differentiating mechanic to ship a generic-shape TW is a known kill move.**

**Sanctum 2 (Coffee Stain, 2013).** Cause: slow attrition (not a hard death, but instructive). Currently ~37 CCU. Studio pivoted to Goat Simulator and Satisfactory; Sanctum 3 indefinitely shelved. Demonstrates that even successful, polished hybrid (FPS+TD) titles erode without ongoing content/community investment, and that a one-and-done shipped TW becomes a long-tail with no live community.

## Open questions for the PM

1. **Which death pattern is the AtoA pivot most exposed to?** Reading the morgue, the pivot's largest exposure is **Bucket A (monetization-mode mismatch)** at the Promise-3 layer — Battle Pass + Ranked are the same shaped surface that killed OMD!U and Dungeon Defenders 2 when applied to co-op/PvE-flavoured games. Promise-1's "all seven mode-types complete at launch" specifically inoculates against Buckets B (pivot whiplash) and D (underbaked launch), which is the sharpest defensive posture in the corpus. The two unmitigated exposures are A (monetization shape) and H (lobby substrate cost) — H being the *expensive* part of Promise-2.

2. **How offline-playable does the AtoA core need to be?** Bucket C (server-rot) suggests a hard rule: no progression, achievements, or single-player content gated behind always-online. Is the PM willing to commit to "campaign + custom games + commander progression playable offline forever" as a launch promise?

3. **What is the credibility mechanism for Promise-3's "without compromising regular play" clause?** Dungeon Defenders 2 demonstrates that even good intentions get reversed under revenue pressure. Is there appetite for a public, written, dated commitment in `decisions/` that becomes binding?

4. **Does the seven-mode-type promise survive the Defenders-of-Ardania test?** Each of the seven mode-types must individually clear "this would be a respectable standalone shipped TW." Is there a per-mode-type ship-bar gate planned before Phase 5?

5. **How is custom-lobby/host substrate (Promise-2) self-funded long-term?** WC3 customs worked because Blizzard ate the host cost. Standalone TWs that try to reproduce the substrate (Tower Wars, Always Outnumbered) have died. Is the plan P2P-host-driven (no central servers), Steam-lobby-piggybacked, or something else?
